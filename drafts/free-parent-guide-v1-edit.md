## The test you're not passing (and why it isn't your fault)

Maybe it was the eye roll. The slammed door. The flat *whatever* when you asked about their day.

Or maybe it's quieter — the realization that you can't remember the last time your teenager looked you in the eye. That the kid who used to run to you when they were hurt now locks their bedroom door.

Here's something about ruptures with your teenager: if you're not having them, you're probably not in a real relationship with them. Blow-ups, silent treatments, slammed doors — they're normal. They're necessary.

Your teen is *supposed* to push against you. That's how they figure out who they are — and at the same time learn whether the people who love them will still show up when things get messy.

What most parenting advice won't tell you:

Your teenager isn't pushing you away because they need space. They're pushing you away because they're testing something far more primal:

> **"Will you stay when I'm unlovable?"**

Think about it:

- When your teen is rude, do you withdraw? *(They register: love is conditional.)*
- When your teen is struggling, do you fix it? *(They register: my feelings are too much for you.)*
- When your teen is angry, do you get angry back? *(They register: you can't handle me.)*

You're not doing anything wrong. You're doing what your own nervous system learned to do when *you* were a teenager.

But here's the invisible machinery underneath: your teen's rejection activates *your* attachment wounds. And your response to their behavior is actually your response to your own unresolved pain.

When your teen slams the door, you're not just dealing with a defiant adolescent. You're dealing with the part of you that learned *anger means abandonment*, or *I have to earn love by being good*, or *emotional messiness is dangerous*. And your nervous system goes into threat mode — exactly as it was trained to do.

## Your nervous system is the problem (and the solution)

You cannot offer your teen relational safety when your own nervous system is in threat mode.

Most parents I work with try to solve this from the neck up — reading parenting books, learning communication techniques, practicing "I statements." And they're confused when none of it works.

Here's why: your teen's nervous system is reading *yours*. Not your words. Not your intentions. Your state.

### Where are you on the ladder?

Think of your nervous system as a ladder with three rungs.

**Top rung — Safe & Social.** Regulated, present, curious. You can think clearly and feel your feelings. Body relaxed, breath easy. *This is where parenting actually happens.* You can say things like: *"I can see you're really upset. Tell me what's going on." "That must have been hard." "I'm here. I'm not going anywhere."*

**Middle rung — Fight or Flight.** Activated, reactive, urgent. Heart racing, jaw tight. You're trying to control or defend; everything feels like an emergency. *This is where most parent-teen conflict lives.* You hear yourself say: *"Don't you dare talk to me that way!" "This ends NOW." "What is WRONG with you?"*

**Bottom rung — Shutdown.** Numb, exhausted, hopeless. You can't think clearly. You've given up. *This is where burnout lives.*

You cannot offer safety, attunement, soothing, delight, or support from the bottom two rungs. It's neurobiologically impossible — when you're in fight/flight or shutdown, the part of your brain responsible for empathy and emotional regulation goes offline.

So the first job isn't changing your teen.

The first job is noticing where *you* are on the ladder.

If you weren't offered safety when *you* were dysregulated as a kid, you don't have the wiring to offer it easily now. Your system learned: big feelings are dangerous. Control them. Shut them down. Get away.

This is not a character flaw. It's neurobiology. And nervous systems can change.

## What your teen really needs: the five conditions

After years of clinical work, I've distilled secure attachment down to five essential conditions. These aren't techniques. They're relational presences you become.

**1. Safety** — *"Can I be myself — messy, angry, failing, changing — and still belong to you?"*
Safety is you staying regulated when they're not. Proof you won't abandon them when they're hard to love.

**2. Attunement** — *"Do you see me? The real me, not the version you wish I was?"*
Seeing beneath the behavior to the feelings underneath, and reflecting them back without judgment. When teens feel attuned to, they relax. When they feel fixed, they defend.

**3. Soothing** — *"Can you stay calm when I'm in chaos?"*
Soothing isn't making feelings go away. It's lending your calm nervous system to your teen when theirs is overwhelmed. Real soothing is presence.

**4. Expressed delight** — *"Do you actually like me? Not just love me — enjoy who I am?"*
Not praise for what they do. Genuine enjoyment of who they are. Teens are starving for this.

**5. Support for exploration** — *"Can I become who I'm meant to be and still come home to you?"*
When the other four are in place, your teen can take risks, make mistakes, figure out who they are — because they know you're the secure base they can return to.

## The REAL method: your way back after rupture

Rupture is inevitable. You're going to lose your ground. You're going to react from your wound instead of your wisdom.

That's not the problem. The problem is when you don't repair.

### R — Regulate
**Come back to your center before reaching out.**

You can't repair from a dysregulated state. If you're still activated, any attempt at repair will land as threat.

Use your breath. Slow inhale, longer exhale. Hand on heart or belly. Notice what's being triggered. Tell yourself: *"I can choose how I respond."*

This isn't about being calm. It's about being regulated enough to reconnect.

### E — Explore
**Get curious about what just happened — without blame.**

Most parents skip ahead to fixing or defending. Don't.

> *"I noticed things got tense when I brought up curfew."*
> *"Something shifted between us. Can we talk about it?"*
> *"I said something that hurt you. Help me understand what landed wrong."*

You're not explaining yourself. You're exploring what happened with genuine curiosity.

### A — Attune
**Validate your teen's emotional reality — even if their behavior needs to change.**

This is the hardest part for most parents, because attunement *feels* like agreement. It isn't.

Attunement says: *"Your feelings make sense, even if your behavior doesn't."*

> *"I imagine that felt disappointing."*
> *"I get how this is frustrating — you probably wanted more freedom."*
> *"That sounded hurtful. I'm listening."*

You're not saying they were right to yell. You're saying you see what they were feeling underneath the yelling.

Connection over correction. Always.

### L — Lean back in
**Re-enter the relationship with warmth and steadiness.**

This is where trust gets built — not through performance, but through genuine re-engagement.

Stay physically present. Don't retreat.

> *"I'm still here. I love you. We'll figure this out."*
> *"Even when we fight, I'm not going anywhere."*
> *"I messed up back there. And I'm coming back because you matter to me."*

Then you stay. Even if they don't respond. Even if they're still mad.

You lean back in.

---

### A real example

Your teen yelled "I hate you!" and you yelled back. You both stormed off.

**Regulate.** Before you knock on their door, you pause. Tightness in your chest, the shame, the urge to defend yourself. You breathe. You find your ground. Then you knock. This can take a few minutes. Don't rush it.

**Explore.** They let you in (barely). You say: *"I noticed we both got really heated back there. I'm curious what happened for you. What did I say that set you off?"*

**Attune.** They snap: *"You never listen to me! You just make all the decisions!"* Instead of defending, you say: *"I hear you. It probably does feel like I'm not listening. That must be really frustrating."*

**Lean back in.** *"I'm sorry I yelled. That wasn't okay. And even when we're both heated, I'm not leaving. We're going to figure this out together. I love you."*

Then you stay. Even if they're still mad. If they need space, you can give it — but space isn't the same as leaving. You come back. Later can be ten minutes or the next day. What matters is that you signal: I'm interested in repair, and I'm staying connected.

## What repair teaches your teen

When you use REAL consistently, your teen learns:

- You can be angry and still loved.
- You can mess up and still belong.
- You can come back together, even after it all falls apart.

That isn't just good parenting. That's modeling how relationships work for the rest of their life.

## A final word

Your teen is pushing you away because they're asking the most important question of their life:

> **"Will you stay when I'm unlovable?"**

The answer you give — not with your words, but with your presence — will shape the rest of their life.

You still have time. Adolescence is a second chance. The attachment window is open.

Stay REAL: Regulate, Explore, Attune, Lean back in.

Your teen is worth it. And so are you.
