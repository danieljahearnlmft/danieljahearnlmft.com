# Substack Post v2 — Expressed Delight (Tuesday publish)

> Daniel's revision. Voice rules clean. Near-final. Issues flagged below the draft.

---

A father sat into a men's group and talk about his fifteen-year-old. He's a good kid.

He listed all the evidence. Honor roll. Sports. Volunteers at the food bank with his friends. Nobody ever calls about him from the school, different from when he was a kid, he said this a few times. His friends are good kids too.

I asked him: when's the last time your son saw you light up when he walked into a room.

He went quiet. For a long time. The whole room shuffled a bit.

That silence is the thing I want to talk about.

Most parents of teenagers are doing more praising than ever before. The performance feedback is constant. Good job on the test. Proud of you for finishing that project. Great game tonight. You're working so hard.

Some of this is genuine. Some anxious: a parent trying to make sure a kid who is starting to seem far away knows they're still seen. Some of it is on the to-do list of parenting 2026. Whether you feel like you are raising lions or raising snowflakes. There is a lot of talk from parent to teen. A lot of praise.

But praise is not the same thing as delight.

And teens know the difference in their body before their mind can name it.

Expressed Delight is one of the five conditions of secure attachment in the Integrative Attachment Therapy model: the framework I trained in with Daniel P. Brown and David Elliott. Of the five conditions:

Safety. Attunement. Soothing. Expressed Delight. Exploration.

It's the one most underestimated in mainstream parenting content. Most attachment writing leans hard on safety and attunement. Delight gets treated like a nice-to-have.

It isn't.

Delight is the felt sense, in your teen's nervous system, that their existence is welcomed. Not their grades. Not their effort. Not their behavior. Them.

Brown and Elliott put it this way in their model: when delight is missing, the self forms around performance instead of essence. The kid develops an identity built on what they produce, because that's the part of them that got received. The being underneath stays hidden, because nobody ever asked it to come out.

This is why so many high-achieving teens are hollow. They aren't lying about doing well. They actually are doing well. But the part of them that's doing well is the only part that's ever been wanted, and they are starting to suspect that the rest of them is not. When you meet a teen who is checking every box and still feels see-through its off over praise, no delight.

So if delight isn't praise, what is it.

It's the look on your face when they walk into the room.

It's "I love the way your mind works."

It's "You make me laugh every time."

It's actually looking at them. Hi. It's you.

It's noticing the specific weird thing about your kid that you would not trade for anything: the way they argue with about the TV remote; the way they hum when they're concentrating; the way they care about animals more than most adults do. The way the know the names of every baseball player. They know the best boots for the best jeans. Things you do not know. Even if you think you do. Feeling that is delight.

What delight is not:

Praise for performance. Grades, sports, college admissions.

Conditional approval. "I'm proud of you when you do x."

Managed tolerance. "Fine. Whatever. Go do your thing."

The absence of criticism, mistaken for warmth.
Please read that again.

A lot of parents I work with are doing the fourth one. They've stopped criticizing. They think that's the same as being a warm presence.

It is not.

Silence isn't delight. Restraint isn't delight. Delight is active. Delight has a face.

Here is the hard part.

Most parents in my office cannot easily express delight in their teenager because nobody ever expressed it in them. You can't easily give what you didn't receive. The body has to learn the shape of it first.

This is why the work is slower than parenting advice makes it sound. You don't read this essay and then start doing it tonight. You read this essay and then notice, this week, how often your face stays neutral when your kid walks in. You notice the small reflex of correction that arrives first, before warmth. You notice that the warmth has been there all along; you just haven't been letting it show.

The contemplative traditions I've practiced in for many years describe this as awareness; attachment psychology describes it as attunement to the self before attunement to the other. Same instruction, two languages. The body has to know the feeling before it can give it.

One thing to try this week.

The next time your teenager walks into the room: stop whatever you're doing. Look up. Let your face change.

Don't say anything if nothing comes. The look on your face is the whole intervention.

That is the practice.

I built a free daily version of it at waybackhome.app; the chapter this piece is drawn from is in *The Way Back Home*.

But honestly: before any of that, just look up.

Daniel

### Awkward sentences (suggested rewrites)

**The "checking every box" close** is the strongest insight in the whole piece but the current sentence trips. Two options:

> "When you meet a teen who is checking every box and still feels see-through, that's the gap. Over-praised. No delight."

> "When a teen checks every box and still feels see-through, this is what's missing. Praise without delight."

**The "Nobody ever calls" sentence** has a comma splice. Suggested:

> "Nobody ever calls about him from the school. Different from when he was a kid, he said. He said it a few times."

(Same content, lets each beat land.)

### Strategic flag (your call, not a typo)

> "Some of it is on the to-do list of parenting 2026. Whether you feel like you are raising **lions or raising snowflakes**."

The lions/snowflakes pairing is loaded — it's coded language from Jordan Peterson / right-coded parenting discourse. Some of your readers will read it as you signaling tribally; others won't notice. If you want both audiences to stay with you, consider neutralizing:

> "Whether you think the kids today are too coddled or too pushed, there is a lot of talk from parent to teen. A lot of praise."

Or just cut the whole sentence — the paragraph still works without it.

If you want the edge, leave it.
